# Federal Reserve Bank of New York: Housing Price Growth Expectations Survey — 2024

## Survey Overview

The Federal Reserve Bank of New York's Center for Microeconomic Data released its 2024 SCE Housing Survey tracking expectations for home prices, rent, mortgage access, and homeownership aspirations.

## Home Price Expectations

- Average **one-year-ahead home price growth** expectations increased to **5.1%** in February 2024 (up from 2.6% in February 2023)
- **Five-year-ahead home price growth** expectations also increased, reflecting sustained bullish sentiment on residential real estate

## Rent Price Expectations

- Rent price growth expectations for the next year increased by **1.5 percentage points** to **9.7%**
- This is the second highest reading in the series since 2022
- Signals continued rental market pressure feeding housing affordability concerns

## Mortgage Accessibility Perceptions

- **74.2%** of renters stated that obtaining a mortgage is "somewhat or very difficult"
- Renters' perceptions about the ease of obtaining a mortgage deteriorated substantially vs. prior year
- This sentiment is consistent with elevated mortgage rates persisting above 6.5% during the period

## Homeownership Aspirations

- Renters' self-assessed probability of ever owning a home decreased by **4.3 percentage points** to **40.1%**
- This reflects a series low — the most pessimistic reading since the survey began
- Driven by affordability constraints, credit access concerns, and high down payment requirements

## Federal Reserve Housing Context (2024)

- Despite Fed interest rate cuts totaling a full percentage point in the latter part of 2024, mortgage rates moved higher
- Reflects the disconnect between Fed policy rates and mortgage rates, which are influenced by:
  - Inflation expectations
  - Treasury yields (30-year mortgages tied to 10-year Treasury)
  - MBS spread dynamics

## RMBS Market Implications

- Persistently high mortgage rates create a "lock-in effect" suppressing turnover in existing pools
- Pessimistic homeownership expectations reduce purchase origination pipeline for non-agency issuance
- Higher rent expectations signal demand durability for built-to-rent (BTR) and multifamily collateral types
- Mortgage access barriers create long-term demand for non-QM/expanded credit products serving creditworthy borrowers who cannot obtain agency financing
