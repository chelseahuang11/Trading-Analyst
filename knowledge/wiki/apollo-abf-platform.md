## Overview

Apollo's Asset-Backed Finance (ABF) platform is the credit engine at the center of the firm's largest asset management strategy by AUM, combining proprietary origination with both private and broadly-syndicated market participation across commercial real estate, residential mortgages, hard assets, and consumer finance. The platform deploys capital through dual channels — direct origination via owned platforms and partnerships, plus secondary market purchases — giving Apollo the flexibility to underwrite at inception or acquire at discount, depending on where risk-adjusted returns are most attractive. RMBS is not a peripheral sleeve but a decade-long core focus: the firm has deployed over $44 billion across the residential mortgage complex, building a track record of minimal losses that underpins its institutional credibility in the asset class.

## Key Insights

The $44 billion RMBS deployment figure takes on strategic significance when set against the $200 billion annualized origination capacity: residential mortgage-related assets represent roughly 22% of Apollo's origination throughput, making RMBS not a niche allocation but a structurally important pillar of the credit platform. The breadth of the RMBS track record — spanning whole loans, non-agency and agency securities, MSR financing, and home equity — signals that Apollo competes across the entire residential capital stack, not just in the securitized tranches that most managers access.

Apollo's dual-channel model — private origination alongside broadly-syndicated ABS market participation — resolves an apparent tension in strategy. Private bilateral deals deliver price certainty, lender alignment, and relationship simplicity: Apollo can commit financing at scale without syndication risk, co-invest alongside originators, and serve as a single counterparty for complex structured solutions. The public ABS market, by contrast, provides liquidity, secondary pricing transparency, and the ability to size up or reduce exposure quickly. Maintaining both capabilities means the desk can originate proprietary transactions when spreads in the public market compress, then rotate into secondary markets when dislocations create discounts to par.

The 2024 global expansion — UK/EU middle-market ABF, Japan, and Korea — is not merely geographic diversification; it is Apollo capitalizing on a structural shift accelerated by Basel III/IV capital requirements. Regulated banks are withdrawing balance sheet from non-agency RMBS warehousing, structured credit origination, and whole-loan financing precisely because these assets carry disproportionate capital charges under the new framework. Apollo is designed to fill that gap: it faces no equivalent regulatory capital constraint, can hold long-duration structured credit across its insurance and credit platforms, and has the origination infrastructure to warehouse and securitize at scale. The bank retrenchment opportunity is therefore durable, not cyclical.

Apollo Asset Backed Credit Company (ABC) packages this platform into a semi-liquid vehicle for wealth and institutional investors, democratizing access to assets that were previously accessible only to large insurance balance sheets or pension funds. The leadership team signals institutional commitment: Bret Leas (Global Head of ABF), Michael Paniwozik (President, structured credit specialist), Stuart Rothstein (Board Chair, Real Assets COO), and Nancy De Liban (15+ years at Bear Stearns in structured products) collectively represent a team that has operated through multiple credit cycles and structured finance regimes, including the GFC that wiped out most bank-affiliated ABF desks.

## Relevant to RMBS Trading

A Trading Analyst at Apollo's ABF desk monitors the FRED dashboard indicators precisely because they are the macro inputs that drive residential mortgage complex entry and exit decisions:

- **30-Year Mortgage Rate** (Mortgage Rates tab): Rising rates compress origination volumes, reducing whole-loan supply and shifting the desk toward secondary RMBS purchases; falling rates trigger prepayment risk in agency and non-agency pools, affecting MSR valuations and duration management. Apollo's $44B deployment history means even 25bp rate moves have material impact on portfolio duration and hedge positioning.
- **Delinquency Rates** (Delinquency tab): Non-agency RMBS pricing is directly sensitive to serious delinquency trends. Apollo's minimal-loss track record is partly a function of buying at the right points in the delinquency cycle — delinquency upticks create secondary buying opportunities while signals of deteriorating underwriting tighten Apollo's own origination standards on whole-loan platforms.
- **Housing Starts** (Housing Supply tab): New construction supply affects the velocity of mortgage origination. Persistent housing starts below demand translates into home price support, which underpins collateral quality across the non-agency and home equity books. A supply glut would compress HPA and stress home equity positions.
- **Home Price Index** (Home Price Index tab): HPI is the single most critical variable for non-agency RMBS credit quality — it determines loss-given-default on underwater loans. Apollo's residential book, spanning low-LTV whole loans through higher-yielding non-QM and non-agency RMBS, has different HPI sensitivities across tranches; the HPI dashboard helps the desk assess which parts of the stack are approaching stress thresholds.

In aggregate, these four indicators form the macro housing framework Apollo uses to calibrate ABF exposure — not as individual signals but as a regime indicator: rising rates + rising delinquency + falling starts + flat/declining HPI signals a defensive posture; falling rates + contained delinquency + supply-constrained starts + appreciating HPI creates the conditions where Apollo adds risk and accelerates origination through its proprietary platforms.
