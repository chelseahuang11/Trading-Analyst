# RMBS Market Conditions: Issuance, Delinquency, Housing Supply, and Home Prices
**Last updated:** 2026-05-04
**Sources:** 03-sifma-mbs-statistics-2024.md, 04-sifma-fixed-income-quarterly-1q26.md, 05-sifma-fixed-income-outstanding.md, 06-cotality-delinquency-dec2025.md, 07-mba-delinquency-q4-2025.md, 08-ny-fed-delinquency-geography-2026.md, 12-freddie-mac-outlook-nov-2024.md, 13-freddie-mac-outlook-sep-2024.md, 14-federal-reserve-household-housing-2024.md, 15-ny-fed-housing-price-expectations-2024.md

## Overview

The RMBS market entered 2026 in an unusual configuration: MBS issuance is surging — up 21.6% in 2024 and another 30% YoY through Q1 2026 — while the housing market that typically drives that activity remains near 30-year transaction lows, constrained by the rate lock-in effect and persistent affordability barriers. Delinquency rates are drifting upward from pandemic-era lows, with the headline national figure masking sharp stress at the FHA tier and pronounced geographic divergence between Sun Belt labor-market-stressed metros and more insulated coastal markets. Home prices continue to appreciate nationally, but household expectations surveys and affordability data reveal a widening structural gap that limits supply response and suppresses new origination pipelines.

## Key Insights

### MBS Market Volume

MBS issuance growth (+21.6% in 2024, +30% through Q1 2026) is running far ahead of purchase-market activity, which bottomed at 30-year lows in late 2024. The divergence resolves once you disaggregate issuance by collateral type: agency MBS dominates total issuance volume, and agency origination is heavily weighted toward refinance activity rather than purchase transactions. When the 30-year fixed rate briefly dipped to a two-year low of 6.08% in September 2024 before rebounding above 6.7%, even that modest window triggered a surge in refinance applications — the MBA reported refinances reached 46.4% of total application activity by end of August 2024 ([Freddie Mac](../raw/13-freddie-mac-outlook-sep-2024.md)). In other words, aggregate issuance statistics are being driven by interest-rate-sensitive refinance supply, not by the housing turnover that a sluggish existing-homes market would otherwise suppress.

Agency MBS daily trading volumes ($414.9B ADV in Q1 2026, +16.4% QoQ) dwarf non-agency volumes ($2.1B ADV), reflecting the structural reality that agency paper remains the primary instrument for dealer hedging, duration management, and Fed/bank portfolio activity ([SIFMA](../raw/04-sifma-fixed-income-quarterly-1q26.md), [SIFMA](../raw/03-sifma-mbs-statistics-2024.md)). Non-agency ADV growth (+28% YoY) is proportionally stronger, consistent with renewed investor appetite for spread product and the ongoing build-out of private-label issuance pipelines. The broader fixed income market backdrop reinforces MBS demand: total fixed income trading averaged $1.8 trillion per day in Q1 2026 (+20.2% YoY), with investor flows rotating toward yield across asset classes as rate volatility moderates ([SIFMA](../raw/04-sifma-fixed-income-quarterly-1q26.md)).

### Delinquency Picture

The gap between the Cotality (3.2%) and MBA (4.26%) headline figures is not a contradiction — it reflects different measurement universes. Cotality collects servicer-reported data across a broad cross-section of residential mortgage loans, while the MBA's National Delinquency Survey focuses specifically on one-to-four-unit residential properties and applies its own seasonal adjustment and reporting conventions. The MBA figure is the one most directly tied to RMBS collateral pools given its universe definition. Both series confirm the same directional story: delinquencies drifted modestly higher through 2025 (+28 basis points YoY on the MBA measure), but remain far below the 12% peak recorded in 2010 ([Cotality](../raw/06-cotality-delinquency-dec2025.md), [MBA](../raw/07-mba-delinquency-q4-2025.md)).

The FHA delinquency rate at 11.52% — the highest since Q2 2021 — is the most important stress signal in the current data and warrants separate analysis from the prime/conforming picture ([MBA](../raw/07-mba-delinquency-q4-2025.md)). FHA borrowers skew toward lower-income first-time buyers, and the MBA identifies two structural drivers: expiration of pandemic-era FHA relief options that had held delinquencies artificially low, and labor market disparities disproportionately affecting FHA borrower segments. The NY Fed data provides supporting evidence — delinquency rates in the lowest-income zip codes surged from approximately 0.5% in 2021 to nearly 3.0% by late 2025, while the highest-income areas remained "largely insulated from these pressures" ([NY Fed](../raw/08-ny-fed-delinquency-geography-2026.md)). This income stratification matters for RMBS collateral analysis: government-insured loan concentrations in subordinated non-agency tranches carry greater exposure to FHA-tier stress.

Geographic stress is concentrated but meaningful in scope: approximately 184 of 384 U.S. metropolitan areas experienced year-over-year delinquency increases ([Cotality](../raw/06-cotality-delinquency-dec2025.md)). The most acute increases occurred in Texas metros — Odessa (+1.3 pp), Laredo (+0.9 pp), and McAllen-Edinburg-Mission (+0.6 pp) — along with Maryland (+0.4 pp statewide) and Nevada (+0.3 pp statewide) ([Cotality](../raw/06-cotality-delinquency-dec2025.md)). The NY Fed's labor market analysis confirms the mechanism: counties experiencing unemployment increases of 1.6 or more percentage points saw delinquency rises of approximately 0.6 percentage points, versus 0.2 percentage points in stable-labor counties ([NY Fed](../raw/08-ny-fed-delinquency-geography-2026.md)). Sun Belt and energy-dependent metros that saw rapid pandemic-era population and home price growth are now experiencing simultaneous labor market normalization and affordability-driven transaction slowdowns, creating a compound stress dynamic. Coastal markets with more stable employment bases and less extreme pandemic-era price appreciation show greater resilience.

### Housing Supply & Construction

Existing home sales at 3.84 million (October 2024) mark the lowest level since October 2010 — a 14-year transaction trough driven primarily by the rate lock-in effect rather than demand destruction ([Freddie Mac](../raw/12-freddie-mac-outlook-nov-2024.md)). Homeowners who financed at 2.5–3.5% during 2020–2021 face an implicit cost of $38,000–$42,000 to move (Freddie Mac's rate lock-in cost estimate, which increased from $38,000 in September to $42,000 by October 2024), suppressing listing activity even as buyer demand persists at current affordability levels. Inventory rose to 4.3 months' supply in September 2024 — the highest since October 2020 — but remains below the 5–6 months considered a balanced market, reflecting the asymmetry between sellers unwilling to move and properties that are gradually cycling through ([Freddie Mac](../raw/12-freddie-mac-outlook-nov-2024.md)).

New construction has not filled the gap. Housing starts declined to a SAAR of 1.24 million, 6.8% below prior-period levels, with multifamily construction contracting over 15% year-over-year ([Freddie Mac](../raw/12-freddie-mac-outlook-nov-2024.md), [Freddie Mac](../raw/13-freddie-mac-outlook-sep-2024.md)). Homebuilder confidence sat at index levels of 39–43, both below the 50 threshold indicating negative near-term building conditions — signaling that builders anticipate insufficient buyer traffic to justify ramping supply ([Freddie Mac](../raw/13-freddie-mac-outlook-sep-2024.md)). The structural supply deficit is therefore likely to persist: locked-in existing owners are not selling, new construction is not accelerating, and the affordability wedge continues to suppress market-clearing transaction volumes. The Federal Reserve's household survey confirms the demand-side constraint: over two-thirds of renters reported being unable to afford a down payment, and 49% could not manage monthly mortgage payments, pointing to a buyer pool that is structurally excluded from homeownership at current rates and price levels ([Federal Reserve](../raw/14-federal-reserve-household-housing-2024.md)).

### Home Prices & Affordability

Despite transaction volume at multi-decade lows, home prices continue to appreciate. The FHFA House Price Index posted 4.2% annual growth as of August 2024 (moderating from 5.1% YoY in June 2024), supported by the same inventory constraint that suppresses turnover — sellers have pricing power even in thin markets when competing listings are scarce ([Freddie Mac](../raw/12-freddie-mac-outlook-nov-2024.md), [Freddie Mac](../raw/13-freddie-mac-outlook-sep-2024.md)). Consumer expectations data reinforces the bullish price sentiment: the NY Fed's 2024 SCE Housing Survey found one-year-ahead home price growth expectations rising to 5.1% (from 2.6% a year prior), with five-year expectations also increasing — suggesting households see appreciation as durable rather than cyclical ([NY Fed](../raw/15-ny-fed-housing-price-expectations-2024.md)).

The affordability picture, however, is deteriorating in the absolute sense. Recent buyers in 2023–2024 carried median monthly mortgage payments of $2,020 nationally and $3,220 in the West — roughly 34% higher than the $1,500 median for all existing mortgage holders ([Federal Reserve](../raw/14-federal-reserve-household-housing-2024.md)). Rent expectations surged to 9.7% growth expected over the next year — the second highest reading in the NY Fed survey series — meaning the rental alternative to ownership is also becoming less accessible ([NY Fed](../raw/15-ny-fed-housing-price-expectations-2024.md)). The homeownership rate declined to 65.6% in Q3 2024 from 66.0% a year prior, with renter-occupied units growing 1.1 million versus only 0.6 million net new owner-occupied units — a structural shift toward renting that reflects affordability exclusion rather than preference change ([Freddie Mac](../raw/12-freddie-mac-outlook-nov-2024.md)). Only 38% of adults reported being satisfied with housing costs in their neighborhoods, with renters the most dissatisfied at fewer than 30% ([Federal Reserve](../raw/14-federal-reserve-household-housing-2024.md)). The renters' self-assessed probability of ever owning a home fell to 40.1% — a series low — underscoring that affordability constraints are reshaping long-term housing formation expectations, not just near-term transaction behavior ([NY Fed](../raw/15-ny-fed-housing-price-expectations-2024.md)).

## Relevant to RMBS Trading

The four dashboard indicators map directly onto the market conditions documented across these sources:

- **Mortgage Rates tab:** The rate lock-in effect is the central organizing feature of the current housing market. The September 2024 dip to 6.08% — briefly the lowest in two years — produced a visible refinance surge (46.4% of applications), which in turn contributed to the issuance boom that produced 2024's $1.6 trillion in MBS volume. Each basis-point move in the 30-year fixed rate shifts the refinance-eligible population and directly affects prepayment speeds on seasoned agency pools. Above-6% rates that persist suppress housing turnover, compressing purchase origination and extending duration on existing MBS collateral.

- **Delinquency tab:** The MBA's 4.26% headline (Q4 2025) and the FHA tier at 11.52% are the primary RMBS credit inputs. The income- and geography-based stratification documented by the NY Fed — lowest-income zip codes at ~3.0% delinquency vs. insulated high-income areas — maps onto collateral quality differentiation across RMBS deal types. Deals with high FHA or government-insured concentrations, or with collateral pools weighted toward lower-income Sun Belt geographies, warrant wider credit spread assumptions than prime jumbo or high-FICO non-QM deals. The transition rate (current → 30 days past due) declining to 0.7% signals that new delinquency formation is stabilizing even as the stock remains elevated.

- **Housing Supply tab:** The housing starts SAAR of 1.24 million and homebuilder confidence below 50 signal a supply-constrained market that will persist through at least the near-term forecast horizon. Persistent supply deficits underpin collateral values across the residential mortgage complex — for RMBS analysis, this translates into support for LTV ratios and loss-given-default assumptions across the non-agency book. However, the multifamily construction contraction (15%+ YoY) is a two-edged signal: it tightens rental supply (supporting rent growth, BTR collateral), while also reducing the new-issuance pipeline of multifamily RMBS collateral.

- **Home Price Index tab:** FHFA at +4.2% (August 2024) and consumer expectations at +5.1% for the year ahead reflect a price regime that supports collateral values and loss assumptions in existing RMBS pools. National home price appreciation at 1.0% YoY as of November 2025 (NY Fed data, a more recent reading) shows deceleration, and Gulf Coast Florida is explicitly flagged as an area with pronounced home price declines correlated with rising delinquencies — a geographic concentration risk factor for deals with Sun Belt exposure.
